# Personal Reflection
**Topic 2 — New Skills Required in the AI Era**

**Course: SWE101 | Assignment 1 and 2**

---

Before starting this research, I was excited about AI but also a little worried about how it might affect software engineering jobs. I knew that AI was changing many industries, but I did not fully understand how deeply it was already affecting the way developers work. I believed it would change things in the future. What this research showed me is that the change is already happening right now.

What surprised me the most was how important skills like prompt engineering, adaptability, and critical thinking are becoming. I honestly did not expect these skills to matter so much. I always thought that coding ability was the most important thing for a software engineer. But after reading articles from researchers at Google, MIT, and universities around the world, I realized that knowing how to work alongside AI — and how to evaluate what it produces — is becoming just as important as writing code itself. The Schulhoff et al. (2024) paper especially surprised me. I did not know that there are over 58 different prompting techniques. That showed me that prompt engineering is a real technical discipline, not just typing questions into a chatbot.

My biggest concern after doing this research is that some entry-level software engineering tasks may become automated. This worries me because junior developers learn by doing those tasks. If AI takes them over, new developers might struggle to build the foundational knowledge they need. Kam et al. (2025) specifically warned about this risk of deskilling — where developers start relying on AI so much that they stop truly understanding the code they are working with. That finding stayed with me because I am at the beginning of my own career. I do not want to become dependent on tools I do not fully understand.

Out of all the skills discussed in this research, I believe adaptability is the most important one for me personally. Technology and AI are changing very quickly. No single skill or tool will stay relevant forever. What will always matter is the ability to learn, adjust, and keep growing. The Li (2022) paper made this point clearly — lifelong learning is not optional anymore, it is a strategic necessity. I think this applies directly to me as a student. The tools I learn today may be completely different in five years, but the habit of learning will always be useful.

At the same time, I see AI more as an opportunity than a threat. It can help developers work faster, reduce repetitive work, and focus on more complex and creative problems. The research showed that AI raises productivity most for engineers who already have strong foundations. That means the answer is not to avoid AI, but to build the skills that make you a better user of it. Engineers who understand systems, think critically, and communicate well will be the ones who benefit most from AI tools.

Going forward, my personal plan is to keep learning new technologies and improve my AI-related skills alongside my core software engineering fundamentals. I want to use AI tools responsibly — meaning I will always try to understand what the code does, not just accept what AI generates. I also want to focus on strengthening my problem-solving and critical thinking abilities, because those are the skills that are hardest to automate and most valuable in the long run. This research did not make me less excited about software engineering. It made me more focused about what I need to work on.
